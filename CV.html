Cardiovascular Physiology


Circuitry of the Cardiovascular System
At which site is systolic blood pressure the highest?






Answer:

Pressures on the venous side of the circulation (e.g. central vein, right atrium, renal vein) are lower than pressures on the arterial side. Pressure in the pulmonary artery and all pressures on the right side of the heart are much lower than their counterparts on the left side of the heart. In the systemic circulation, sytolic pressure is actually slightly higher in the downstream arteries (e.g. renal artery) than in the aorta because of the reflection of pressure waves at branch points.

The greatest pressure decrease in the circulation occurs across the arterioles because





Answer:

The decrease in pressure at any level of the CV system is caused by the resistance of the blood vessels (δP=QxR) The greater the resistance, the greater the decrease in ressure. The arterioles are the site of highest resistance in the vasculature. The capillaries have the greatest surface and cross-sectional area. Velocity of blood flow is lowest in the capillaries.

Hemodynamics
A 53-year-old woman is found, by arteriography, to have 50% narrowing of her left renal artery. What is the expected change in blood flow through the stenotic artery?






Answer:

D. If the radius of the artery decreased by 50%, then resistance would increase by 24 or 16 (R - 8νl/πr4). Because blood flow is inversely proportional to resistance, flow will decrease to one sixteenth of the original value.

When a person moves from a supine to standing position, which of the following compensatory changes occurs?






Answer:

B. When a person moves to a standing position, blood pools in the leg veins, causing decreased venous return to the heart, decreased cardiac output, and decreased arterial pressure. The baroreceptors detect the decrease in arterial pressure and activate the vasomotor center to increase sympathetic and decrease parasympathetic outflow. There is an increase in heart rate, decreasing PR interval, contractility, and total peripheral resistance. Because both heart rate and contractility are increased, cardiac output will increase towards normal.

The tendency for blood flow to be turbulent is increased by






Answer:

Turbulent flow is predicted when the Reynolds number is increased. Factors that increase the Reynolds number and produce turbulent flow are decreased viscosity (hematocrit) and increased velocity. Partial occlusion of a blood vessel increases the Reynolds number and turbulence because the decrease in cross-sectional area results in increased blood velocity.

Pulse pressure is






Answer:

Pulse pressure is the difference between the highest and lowest arterial pressures. It reflects the stroke volume. Pulse pressure increases when the capacitance of the arteries decreases, such as in aging.

Cardiac Electrophysiology
A person's ECG has no P wave, but has a normal QRS complex and a normal T wave. Therefore, his pacemaker is located in the






Answer:

The absent P wave indicates that the atrium is not depolarizing and therefore, the pacemaker cannot be in the SA node. Because the QRS and T waves are normal, depolariation and repolarization of the ventricle must be proceeding in the normal sequence. This situation can occur if the pacemaker is located in the AV node. If the pacemaker were located in the bundle of His or in the Purkinje system, the ventricles would activate in an abnormal sequence, depending on the exact location of the pacemaker, and the QRS wave would have an abnormal configuration. Ventricular muscle does not have pacemaker properties.

An ECG on a person shows ventricular extrasystoles. The extrasystolic beat would produce:






Answer:

On the extrasystolic beat, pulse pressure decreases because there is inadequate ventricular filling time- the ventricle beats too soon. As a result, stroke volume decreases.

In the SA node, phase 4 depolarization (pacemaker potential) is attributable to:





Answer:

Phase 4 depolarization is responsible for the pacemaker property of SA node cells. It is caused by an increase in Na+ conductance and an inward Na+ current (If which depolarizes the cell membrane.

After an extrasystole, the next "normal" ventricular contraction produces:






Answer:

The postextrasystolic contraction produces increased pulse pressure because contractility is increased. Extra Ca++ enters the cell during the extrasystolic beat. Contractility is directly related to the amount of intracellular Ca++ available for binding to troponin C.

The ventricles are completely depolarized during which isoelectric portion of the ECG?






Answer:

The PR segment and SR segment are the only portions of the ECG that are isoelectric. The PR interval includes the P wave (atrial depolarization) and the PR segment, which represents conduction through the AV node. During this phase, the ventricles are not yet depolarized. The ST segment is the only isoelectric period when the entire ventricle is depolarized.

A 30-year-old female patient's ECG shows two P waves preceding each QRS complex. The interpretation of this pattern is:






Answer:

D. A pattern of 2 P waves preceding each QRS complex indicates that only every other P wave is conducted through the AV node to the ventricle. Thus, conduction velocity through the AV node must be decreased.

Which of the following is the result of an inward Na+ current?






Answer:

B. The upstroke of the AP in the atria, ventricles, and Purkinje fibers is the result of a fast inward Na+ current. The upstroke of the AP in the SA node is the result of an inward Ca++ current. The plateau of the ventricular AP is the result of a slow inward Ca++ current. Repolarization in all cardiac tissues is the result of an outward K+ current.

The physiologic function of the relatively slow conduction through the AV node is to allow sufficient time for






Answer:

The AV delay, which corresponds to the PR interval, allows time for filling of the ventricles from the atria. If the ventricles contracted befor they were filled, stroke volume would decrease.

The low-resistance pathways between myocardial cells that allow for the spread of action potentials are the





Answer:

The gap junctions at the intercalated discs between cells are the low-resistance sites of current spread.

Cardiac Muscle and Cardiac Output
If the ejection fraction increases, there will be a decrease in






Answer:

An increase in ejection fraction means that a higher fraction of the end-diastolic volume is ejected in the stroke volume (e.g. because of the administration of a positive inotropic agent). When this situation occurs, the volume remaining in the ventricle after systole, the end-systolic volume will be reduced. Cardiac output, pulse pressure, stroke volume, and systolic pressure will be increased.

An increase in contractility is demonstrated on a Frank-Starling diagram by:






Answer:

An increase in contractility produces an increase in cardiac output for a given end-diastolic volume, or pressure. The Frank-Starling relationship demonstrates the matching of cardiac output with venous return. An increase in contractility (positive inotropic effect) will shift the curve upward.

Myocardial contractility is best correlated with the intracellular concentration of






Answer:

Contractility of myocardial cells depends on the intracellular [Ca++] which is regulated by Ca++ entry across the cell membrane during the plateau of the AP and by Ca++ uptake into and release from the SR. Ca++ binds to troponin C and removes the inhibition of actin-myosin interaction, allowing contraction (shortening) to occur.

Cardiac output of the right side of the heart is what percentage of the cardiac output of the left side of the heart?






Answer:

Cardiac output of the left and right sides of the heart is equal. Blood ejected from the left side of the heart to the systemic circulation must be oxygenated by passage through the pulmonary circulation.

Which of these points represents the normal operating point of the heart?






Answer:

2 represents the normal operating point of the heart, or the intersection between blood volume and cardiac output. 1 represents the operating point under sympathetic stimulation, 3 represents the operating point under sedation, and 4 represents mean systemic pressure.

Which receptor mediates slowing of the heart?






Answer:

Acetylcholine causes slowing of the heart via muscarinic receptors in the SA node.

Which of the following agents or changes has a negative inotropic effect on the heart?






Answer:

A negative inotropic effect is one that decreases myocardial contractility. Contractility is the ability to develop tension at a fixed muscle length. Factors that decrease contractility are those that decrease the intracellular [Ca+] Increasing heart rate increases intracellular [Ca++] because more Ca++ ions enter the cell during the plateau of each action potential. Sympathetic stimulation and norepinephrine increase intracellular [Ca++] by increasing entry during the plateau and increasing the storage of Ca++ by the SR for later release. Cardiac glycosides increase intracellular [Ca++] by inhibiting the Na+-K+ pump, thereby inhibiting Na+-Ca++ exchange (a mechanism that pumps Ca++ out of the cell). Acetylcholine has a negative inotroic effect on the atria.

Cardiac Cycle

On the graph showing left ventricular volume and pressure, isovolumetric contraction occurs between points






Answer:

Isovolumetric contraction occurs during ventricular systole, before the aortic valve opens. Ventricular pressure increases, but volume remains constant because blood cannot be ejected into the aorta against a closed valve.


The aortic valve closes at point






Answer:

Closure of the aortic valve occurs once ejection of blood from the ventricle has occurred and the left ventricular pressure has decreased to less than the aortic pressure.


The first heart sound corresponds to point






Answer:

The first heart sound corresponds to closure of the atrial-ventricular valves. Before this closure occurs, the ventricle fills (phase D-A) After the valves close, isovolumetric contraction begins and ventricular pressure increases (phase A-B)


If the heart rate is 70bpm, then the cardiac output of this ventricle is closest to:






Answer:

Stroke volume is the volume ejected from the ventricle and is represented on the pressure-volume loop as phase B-C; end-diastolic volume is about 140mL and end-systolic volume is about 65 mL; the difference, or stroke volume, is 75mL. Cardiac output is calculated as stroke volume x heart rate or 75mL x 70 bpm = 5.25L/m.

Inspiration "splits" the second heart sound because






Answer:

The second heart sound is associated with closure of the aortic and pulmonic valves. Because the aortic valve closes before the pulmonic valve, the sound can be split by inspiration.

Aortic pressure reaches its highest level immediately after the rapid ejection of blood during left ventricular systole. This highest level actually coincides with the beginning of the reduced ventricular ejection phase.

During which phase of the cardiac cycle is ventricular volume lowest?







Answer:

Ventricular volume is at its lowest value while the ventricle is relaxed (diastole), just before ventricular filling begins).

During which phase of the cardiac cycle does the mitral valve open?







Answer:

The mitral (atrioventricular) valve opens when left atrial ressure becomes higher than left ventricular pressure. This situation occurs when the left ventricular pressure is at its lowest level- when the ventricle is relaxed, blood has been ejected from the previous cycle, and before refilling has occurred.

A 66-year-old man with chronic hypertension is treated with prazosin by his physician. The treatment successfully decreases his blood pressure to within the normal range. What is the mechanism of the drug's action?












Answer:

J. Prazosin is a specific antagonist of α1 receptors, which are present in vascular smooth muscle, but not in the heart. Inhibition of α1 receptors results in vasodilation of the cutaneous and splanchnic vascular beds, decreased total peripheral resistance, and decreased blood pressure.

Propranolol has which of the following effects?






Answer:

Propranolol, a β-adrenergic antagonist, blocks all sympathetic effects that are mediated by a β1 or β2 receptor. The sympathetic effect on the SA node is to increase HR via a β1 receptor; therefore; propranolol decreases HR. Ejection fraction reflects ventricular contractility, which is another effect of β1 receptors; thus propranolol decreases contractility, ejection fraction, and stroke volume. Splanchnic and cutaneous resistance are mediated by α1 receptors.
