In a hospital error, a 60-yo-woman is infused with large volumes of a solution that causes lysis of her RBCs. The solution was most likely
   
   
   
   
 
   
Answer:

D. Lysis of the patient's RBCs was caused by entry of water and swelling of the cells to the point of rupture. Water would flow into the RBCs if the ECF became hypotonic (lower osmotic pressure) relative to the ICF. Definitionally, isotonic solutions do not cause water to flow in or out of cells because the osmotic pressure is the same on both sides of the cell membrane. Hypertonic solutions would cause shrinkage of the RBCs. 150mM NaCl and 300 mM mannitol are isotonic. 350mM mannitol and 150mM CaCl2 are hypertonic. Because the reflection coefficient of urea is <1.00, 300mM urea is hypotonic.

Solutions A and B are separated by a membrane that is permeable to urea. Solution A is 10mM urea, and solution B is 5mM urea. If the concentration in urea in solution A is doubled, the flux of urea across the membrane will
   
   
   
   
   
   
Answer:

B. Flux is proportional to the concentration difference across the membrane, J = -PA(CA-CB). Originally, CA - CB = 10mM-5mM = 5mM. When the urea concentration was doubled in solution A, the concentration difference became 20mM-5mM = 15mM or 3 times the original difference. Therefore, the flux would also triple. Note that the negative sign preceding the equation is ignored if the lower concentration is subtracted from the higher concentration.

Solutions A and B are separated by a semipermeable membrane. Solution A contains 1mM sucrose and 1mM urea. Solution B contains 1mM sucrose. The reflection coefficient for sucrose is one, and the reflection coefficient for urea is zero. Which of the following statements about these solutions is correct?
   
   
   
   
   
   
Answer:

D. Solution A contains both sucrose and urea at concentrations of 1mM, whereas solution B contains only sucrose at a concentration of 1mM. The calculated osmolarity of solution A is 2mOsm/L, and the calculated osmolarity of solution B is 1mOsm/L. Therefore solution A, which has a higher osmolarity, is hyperosmotic with respect to solution B, but have the same effective osmotic pressure (isotonic) because the only "effective" solute is sucrose, which has the same concentration in both solutions. Urea is not an effective solute because its reflection coefficient is zero.

Diffusion Potential, Resting Membrane Potential, and Action Potential
Solutions A and B are separated by a semipermeable membrane that is permeable to K+ but not to Cl-. Solution A is 100mM KCl, and solution B is 1mM KCl. Which of the following statements about solution A and B are true?
   
   
   
   
   
   
Answer:

D. Because the membrane is permeable only to K+ ions, K+ will diffuse down its concentration gradient from solution A to solution B, leaving some Cl- ions behind in solution A. A diffusion potential will be created, with solution A negative with respect to solution B. Generation of a diffusion potential involves moement of only a few ions and, therefore, does not cause a change in the concentration of the bulk solutions.

Skeletal Muscle
Repeated stimulation of a skeletal muscle fiber causes a contraction (tetanus). Accumulation of which solute in intracellular fluid is responsible for the tetanus?
   
   
   
   
   
   


Answer:

E. During repeated stimulation of a muscle fiber, Ca++ is released from the SR more quickly than it can be reaccumulated. In skeletal muscle, which of the following events occurs before depolarization of the T tubules in the mechanism of excitation-contraction coupling?
   
   
   
   
   
   
Answer:

A. In the mechanism of excitation-contraction coupling, excitation always precedes contraction. Excitation refers to the electrical activation of the muscle cell, which begins with an action potential (depolarization) in the sarcolemmal membrane that spreads to the T tubules. Depolarization of the T tubules then leads to the release of Ca++ from the nearby SR followed by an increase in intracellular concentration, binding of Ca++ to troponin C, and then contraction.

Smooth Muscle

Answer:

C. An elevation of intracellular [Ca++] is common to the mechanism of excitation-contraction coupling in skeletal and smooth muscle. In skeletal muscle, Ca++ binds to troponin C, initiating the cross-bridge cycle. In smooth muscle, Ca++ binds to calmodulin. The Ca++-calmodulin complex activates myosin light chain kinase, which phosphorylates myosin so that shortening can occur. Spontaneous depolarizations and gap junctions are characteristics of unitary smooth muscle but not skeletal muscle.

Temperature Regulation
Pathogens that produce fever cause
   
   
   
   
   
   
Answer:

Pathogens release interleukin-1 from phagocytic cells. IL-1 then acts to increase the production to prostaglandins, ultimately raising the temperature set point in the anterior hypothalamus. When a person moves from a supine to standing position, which of the following compensatory changes occurs?
   
   
   
   
   
   
Answer:

B. When a person moves to a standing position, blood pools in the leg veins, causing decreased venous return to the heart, decreased cardiac output, and decreased arterial pressure. The baroreceptors detect the decrease in arterial pressure and activate the vasomotor center to increase sympathetic and decrease parasympathetic outflow. There is an increase in heart rate, decreasing PR interval, contractility, and total peripheral resistance. Because both heart rate and contractility are increased, cardiac output will increase towards normal.

The tendency for blood flow to be turbulent is increased by
   
   
   
   
   
   
Answer:

Turbulent flow is predicted when the Reynolds number is increased. Factors that increase the Reynolds number and produce turbulent flow are decreased viscosity (hematocrit) and increased velocity. Partial occlusion of a blood vessel increases the Reynolds number and turbulence because the decrease in cross-sectional area results in increased blood velocity.

Pulse pressure is

   
   
   
   
   
Answer:

Pulse pressure is the difference between the highest and lowest arterial pressures. It reflects the stroke volume. Pulse pressure increases when the capacitance of the arteries decreases, such as in aging.

The absent P wave indicates that the atrium is not depolarizing and therefore, the pacemaker cannot be in the SA node. Which of the following agents or changes has a negative inotropic effect on the heart?






Answer:

A negative inotropic effect is one that decreases myocardial contractility. Contractility is the ability to develop tension at a fixed muscle length. Factors that decrease contractility are those that decrease the intracellular [Ca+] Increasing heart rate increases intracellular [Ca++] because more Ca++ ions enter the cell during the plateau of each action potential.
